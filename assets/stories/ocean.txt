	The dunes rose like sleeping giants, their slopes bound by the pale fingers of sea oats. Elias crested the final ridge, his boots sinking into the fine, cool silt. The Atlantic lay before him, a vast expanse of bruised purple and obsidian. It breathed. Each swell pushed a rhythmic sigh against the shore, a sound that bypassed the ears and settled directly in the marrow of his bones.
	A figure stood where the dry sand met the damp. The man wore a heavy knit sweater the color of a storm cloud. He didn't turn as Elias approached. He simply watched the horizon.
	“The tide is hesitant tonight,” the man said. His voice carried the texture of crushed shells.
	Elias stopped a few paces behind him. “Does it have a reason to be?”
	“It waits for the moon to give the signal,” the stranger replied. He gestured toward the horizon where a silver sliver began to bleed light into the water. “I am Kaelen. I watch the boundaries.”
	“I’m just walking,” Elias said. “The city... it doesn't stop. I needed something that did.”
	Kaelen turned. His eyes reflected the first true rays of moonlight, shimmering with an iridescent quality that suggested he had spent more time in the spray than in the sun. “The ocean never stops, Elias. It only changes its pace. Follow the waterline. The sand there holds the memory of the day’s heat, but the water brings the chill of the deep. It balances the blood.”
	Elias stepped forward, his boots clicking against a stray piece of driftwood before he reached the dark, packed sand. The transition felt instantaneous. The ground beneath him firmed up, slick and mirrored. 
	“Why does the water look like ink?” Elias asked.
	“Because it hides the mountains beneath,” Kaelen said, walking beside him with a stride that made no sound. “You walk over valleys a mile deep. You walk over forests of kelp that sway in currents we will never feel. The ink is just a curtain.”
	A wave rolled in, a thin sheet of glass that shattered into white lace at their ankles. The water swirled around Elias’s feet, tugging gently at his heels as it retreated. 
	“It’s trying to take the sand back with it,” Elias noted.
	“It’s reclaiming what it lent to the land,” Kaelen said. “Watch the foam. See the tiny sparks?”
	Elias leaned down. Within the white froth, pinpricks of blue light flickered and died. “Phosphorescence.”
	“Starlight captured in salt,” Kaelen corrected. “The organisms wake up when the water moves. They glow to tell the dark they are still here. Does the weight in your chest feel lighter when you see them?”
	Elias took a long breath. The air tasted of iodine and ancient minerals. “A little. It’s hard to remember the deadlines when the earth is glowing at my feet.”
	“Deadlines are ghosts,” Kaelen said. “The only line that matters is the one between the sky and the sea. Look up.”
	The sky had transitioned from bruised purple to a deep, velvety black. Stars hung like heavy droplets of silver, so bright they seemed to vibrate. The Milky Way smeared across the zenith, a river of ghost-light.
	“I didn't think there were this many,” Elias whispered.
	“The glare of men hides them,” Kaelen said. “But they wait. They have watched the tides rise and fall since the world was molten. They find the silence comforting.”
	“Do you ever get lonely out here?” Elias asked.
	Kaelen laughed, a soft sound like pebbles rolling in a current. “How can one be lonely with a thousand miles of conversation hitting the shore? Every wave brings a message from a different coast. This one,” he pointed to a medium swell that tumbled over itself in a roar of white noise, “started near the Azores. It carries the chill of the deep Atlantic.”
	They continued down the beach. The only sounds were the rhythmic *crush-hiss* of the surf and the distant moan of a buoy. The moonlight intensified, carving a shimmering path across the water—a silver road that led straight to the moon’s core.
	“Can we walk on that?” Elias asked, nodding toward the reflection.
	“In your mind, perhaps,” Kaelen said. “It is the path of the Dreamers. Those who cannot sleep come here to find the road. It doesn't lead to a place, but to a state of being.”
	“I haven't slept well in months,” Elias admitted. His shoulders, usually bunched near his ears, began to drop. 
	“The brain is a hive of bees,” Kaelen said. “You must give them a flower to land on. Focus on the sound of the recession. Listen to the water pull back over the shingles.”
	They reached a stretch of beach covered in small, flat stones. As the waves withdrew, the stones rattled against one another, a melodic, percussive clatter.
	“It sounds like rain on a tin roof,” Elias said.
	“Or the breath of the planet,” Kaelen suggested. “The earth inhales, and the water climbs the sand. It exhales, and the stones sing their song. Match your breath to the stones, Elias.”
	Elias stood still. He drew air in as the wave pushed forward, cooling his shins. He released it as the water dragged the pebbles back toward the dark. *In. Out. In. Out.* The tightness in his temples began to dissolve, replaced by a heavy, cool sensation.
	“Your pulse is slowing,” Kaelen observed. “The sea has a way of resetting the clock.”
	“It feels like the world is much bigger than I thought,” Elias said.
	“It is,” Kaelen replied. “And you are a very small part of it. That is a gift. You don't have to carry the whole thing. The ocean does that for you.”
	They walked until they reached a giant piece of sun-bleached driftwood, a cedar tree that had been stripped of its bark and smoothed by years of salt and sand. It looked like the skeletal remains of a prehistoric beast.
	“Sit,” Kaelen commanded gently.
	Elias sat. The wood was cold but solid. He leaned his head back and looked at the stars. They seemed to be spinning slowly, a carousel of light.
	“Why did you come to find me?” Elias asked, his voice growing thick with a sudden, pleasant lethargy.
	“I didn't find you,” Kaelen said, leaning against the driftwood. “I am a part of the rhythm. When the rhythm finds someone who is out of sync, I appear to help them find the beat again. Do you hear the wind in the dunes?”
	Elias listened. The wind moved through the sea oats, a dry, whispering sound that contrasted with the wet heave of the ocean. It was a soft *shhh*, a constant reminder to be still.
	“It sounds like a lullaby,” Elias whispered.
	“It is the oldest one,” Kaelen said. “The song of the wind and the grass. It tells the story of the sand that was once a mountain, and the shell that was once a home. Everything changes, Elias. Everything moves toward rest.”
	A large wave, larger than the others, surged up the beach. It reached the base of the driftwood, soaking Elias’s boots in a cold, bracing embrace. It didn't feel jarring. It felt like a greeting.
	“The tide is peaking,” Kaelen said. “The world is at its fullest. Soon, it will begin to ebb. That is when the deepest sleep comes.”
	“I feel like I could drift away with it,” Elias said. His eyelids felt like lead. The silver path on the water seemed to broaden, inviting him into the quiet.
	“Then let the anchor go,” Kaelen said. His voice was becoming more distant, blending with the sound of the surf. “The water will hold you. The sand will support you. The stars will watch over the house of your mind.”
	Elias closed his eyes. The image of the moon reflected on the black water remained burned into his retinas, a glowing coin in a dark well. He felt the vibration of the next wave through the wood of the cedar tree.
	“Will you be here when I wake up?” Elias asked.
	“I am always here,” Kaelen’s voice was a whisper now, indistinguishable from the wind in the oats. “In the salt on your skin and the sound in your ears. Sleep now, Traveler. The ocean has the night shift.”
	Elias felt his chin drop toward his chest. The smell of the sea became a warm blanket. The rhythmic *thump* of the waves matched the slow, steady beat of his heart. The silver road was right there, beneath his feet, and he began to walk it, leaving the shore behind for the soft, dark embrace of the deep, quiet night. 
	The waves continued their work. *Crush. Hiss. Silence.* The moon climbed higher, turning the world to chrome. And on the driftwood log, the man let out a final, contented sigh, disappearing into the dream of the tide. 
	The Atlantic exhaled. The world went still. 
	The stars kept their watch. 
	The beach remained, a kingdom of salt and shadow, waiting for the sun but in no hurry for its arrival. Every grain of sand settled. Every ripple smoothed. 
	Peace held the shore. 
	The water retreated one last time, leaving behind a pristine mirror, reflecting the infinite, silent sky. 
	Sleep arrived, as inevitable and gentle as the tide.
