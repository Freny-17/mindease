	The sun had long since dipped below the horizon, leaving behind a sky the color of crushed plums and cooling embers. Elian sat atop the crest of a massive, rolling dune. Beneath him, the sand held the lingering heat of the day, a deep and steady warmth that seeped through his clothes and anchored him to the earth. He ran his hand across the surface. The grains felt like liquid silk, individual diamonds of silica sliding between his fingers in a soft, rhythmic hiss.
	Beside him sat Malik, an old man whose face was a map of the desert itself, etched with fine lines like the wind-carved canyons to the east. Malik didn’t move. He simply watched the indigo deepen.
	"The desert breathes in the heat all day," Malik said, his voice a low, melodic rasp that seemed to vibrate in the still air. "And now, it exhales. Can you feel the shift?"
	Elian closed his eyes and inhaled. The air had lost its sharp, biting dryness. It felt rounder now, carrying the faint, ghostly scent of night-blooming jasmine from some distant, hidden oasis.
	"It feels like the world is slowing down," Elian whispered.
	"The world does not slow," Malik replied, turning his head slightly. "We simply stop trying to outrun it. Here, there is nowhere to go. There is only the dune, the sky, and the breath you take right now."
	A gentle breeze, no more than a phantom’s touch, brushed across the ridge. It didn't howl or whistle. It sang a private song, a low-frequency hum as it reorganized the peaks of the sand. The sound mimicked the distant roll of a calm ocean, a constant, soothing white noise that filled the vast emptiness.
	"Look up, Elian," Malik said, gesturing with a weathered hand toward the zenith. "Tell me what you see, but do not use the names the map-makers gave them."
	Elian tilted his head back. The sky was no longer a flat ceiling. It had become a bottomless ocean of obsidian, layered with depth and shimmering with a billion pinpricks of silver fire. The Milky Way arched overhead, a spilled river of powdered diamonds and violet mist.
	"I see a path of dust," Elian said slowly, his words trailing off as he stared. "It looks like white smoke frozen in the air."
	"It is the breath of the ancestors," Malik murmured. "They watch the sands to ensure they do not wander too far. They keep the silence for us so we may hear our own hearts."
	The silence wasn't an absence of sound, but a presence of its own. It was a heavy, velvety blanket that pressed softly against Elian’s ears. It muffled the frantic rhythms of the distant cities he had left behind. Every few minutes, a grain of sand would succumb to gravity, sliding down the face of the dune with a sound like a tiny, dry waterfall.
	"Why is the air so still?" Elian asked. "Usually, the wind dominates this place."
	Malik leaned back on his elbows, sinking into the soft slope. "The wind is tired, too. Even the air needs to rest. It settles into the hollows. It curls around the roots of the scrub brush. It waits for the moon to give it permission to move again."
	They sat in silence for a long time. Elian watched a shooting star streak across the northern quadrant, a momentary scratch of light that vanished before he could point it out. It left behind a lingering sense of wonder, a reminder of how vast the universe was and how small his own worries had become.
	"The sand is cooling," Elian noted, pressing his palms flat against the earth.
	"The heat is moving deeper," Malik explained. "It retreats to the core of the dune. If you dig your hands down just a few inches, you will find the warmth of noon still waiting for you. It’s a secret the desert keeps."
	Elian dug his fingers into the slope. Malik was right. Beneath the cool, night-kissed surface, the sand remained toasted and snug. It felt like burying his hands in a warm quilt. He let his shoulders drop. The tension he had been carrying in his neck—a tight knot of strings—slowly unraveled.
	"Do you hear the singing?" Malik asked, his voice dropping to a near-whisper.
	Elian strained his ears. At first, there was nothing but the pulse in his own temples. Then, he heard it. A low, resonant vibration coming from deep within the dune. It was a subterranean moan, a harmonic frequency caused by the shifting weight of the sand.
	"It sounds like a giant snoring," Elian said, a small smile tugging at his lips.
	"The dunes are alive," Malik said. "They travel miles every year. They grow, they shrink, they talk to one another in the dark. You are sitting on a traveler that has seen a thousand years. It knows how to be still. It knows how to wait."
	"What is it waiting for?"
	"For the same thing you are," Malik said. "The moment when the mind stops asking questions and the body remembers how to simply exist. Listen to the rhythm, Elian. Match your lungs to the dunes."
	Elian focused on his breathing. He inhaled the cool, starlit air, feeling it expand his chest. He exhaled, feeling his weight sink further into the warm embrace of the sand. With every breath, the horizon seemed to expand, and the stars seemed to grow larger and softer, like glowing lanterns hung from an invisible ceiling.
	"My eyes feel heavy," Elian admitted.
	"That is the desert's gift," Malik replied. "It takes the weight of your thoughts and buries them in the sand. It replaces them with the light of the stars. Let the silver light fill you. There is no need for words now. There is only the cooling air, the warm earth beneath you, and the endless, peaceful dark."
	Elian didn't respond. He didn't need to. He watched the flicker of a distant star, its light pulsing like a slow, steady heartbeat. The breeze whispered one last time, a soft "hush" that traveled across the dunes and vanished into the night. He let his head rest back against the slope, the sand shaping itself perfectly to his form. The desert was wide, the night was long, and he was finally, perfectly, still.
