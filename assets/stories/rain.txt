	The scent of cedar and aged sap clung to the air, thick enough to taste. Outside, the first droplets of the evening struck the tin roof, a metallic stutter that signaled the arrival of the gray clouds. Elias moved toward the hearth, his joints popping like dry kindling. He knelt, the rough wool of his trousers catching on the floorboards, and reached for a stack of split oak.
	"The sky finally broke," he said, his voice a low vibration that seemed to come from the floorboards themselves.
	Elara leaned back into the oversized armchair, the cushions swallowing her weight. The fabric felt like sun-warmed moss against her skin. She watched the way the firelight danced in the reflection of the windowpane, a ghost of gold against the deepening indigo of the forest.
	"I thought it might hold off until dawn," she replied, her voice barely a whisper.
	Elias shook his head, placing a thick log onto the glowing embers. 
	"The hemlocks were bowing too low for that. They knew the weight was coming."
	He struck a match, the sulfurous tang cutting through the sweetness of the wood smoke for a fleeting second. The flame licked the bark, curling into a hungry orange ribbon. He watched it for a moment, his eyes reflecting the miniature inferno.
	"Will the roof hold the sound?" she asked.
	Elias stood, brushing splinters from his palms. 
	"It won’t just hold it. It amplifies the rhythm. Every dent in that metal has a different pitch. Listen."
	A sudden gust swept through the pines, a long, low sigh that rattled the window frames. Then came the rain, no longer a stutter but a steady, rhythmic pulse. *Drum, drum, shiver. Drum, drum, hiss.* The water cascaded off the eaves, hitting the stone rain-barrels with a hollow, metallic thrum.
	"It sounds like a clock with too many hands," Elara said, closing her eyes.
	"It’s a different kind of time out here," Elias agreed.
	He crossed the room to the small kitchenette. The floor groaned under his boots, a familiar, grounded sound that spoke of decades of sturdy construction. He lifted a heavy copper kettle, filling it from the hand pump. The water splashed against the metal, cold and bright.
	"The tea is peppermint and dried clover," Elias announced, the clink of ceramic mugs punctuating his words. "Grown in the clearing behind the shed. It carries the taste of the summer sun, even on a night like this."
	Elara shifted, her hand brushing the velvet throw blanket draped over the arm of the chair. 
	"The fire is breathing," she noted, watching the way the flames pulsed in and out of the hearth.
	"Ignis is a picky tenant," Elias chuckled, referring to the hearth as if it were a living creature. "He prefers the oak. Gives him a steady heartbeat. Pine makes him spit and snap, too much nervous energy."
	The kettle began to whistle, a thin, silver thread of sound that climbed above the low roar of the rain. Elias moved with a deliberate slowness, pouring the water in a steaming arc. The scent of mint exploded into the room, sharp and clean, cutting through the heaviness of the humidity.
	"Hold this," Elias said, handing her a mug.
	The ceramic was coarse, unglazed on the outside, providing a textured grip that warmed her palms instantly. 
	"It’s heavy," Elara remarked.
	"Good clay usually is," Elias said, settling into his own chair across from her. "It reminds you that you’re holding onto something real."
	They sat in silence for a time, the only sounds the crackle of the oak and the relentless, soothing hammer of the rain. The world outside the cabin walls had vanished, replaced by a wall of liquid silver and the deep green shadows of the woods. 
	"Do you ever feel the cabin move?" Elara asked, her eyes drooping.
	Elias took a slow sip of his tea. 
	"She sways, just a fraction. Like a ship at anchor. The wood expands when the damp hits. It’s the house stretching its limbs."
	"I can hear the walls creaking," she murmured.
	"That's just the cedar settling into the grooves," Elias explained. "The rain presses down, and the foundation pushes back. It’s a conversation they’ve been having for fifty years."
	The rain intensified, a sudden sheet of water slamming against the north wall. The sound was a roar now, a white noise that washed away thought and replaced it with a primal sense of sanctuary. The glass panes vibrated, yet the warmth of the fire remained a constant, unmoving shield.
	"The storm sounds angry," Elara said, though she didn't move.
	"Not angry," Elias corrected. "Just busy. It has a lot of earth to soak before the sun comes back."
	He reached out and stirred the fire with a long iron poker. Sparks swirled up the chimney, a constellation of dying stars. The heat reached out and brushed Elara’s cheeks, a soft, invisible hand.
	"Does the rain ever stop?" she asked, her voice trailing off.
	"Eventually," Elias said. "But for now, it’s doing the work for you. It’s washing the day into the soil. You don't have to carry any of it inside these walls."
	Elara felt the tension in her shoulders dissolve, melting like wax near the hearth. The rhythm of the roof became her own pulse. *Drum, drum, shiver. Drum, drum, sleep.*
	"The blankets are heavy," she whispered, her fingers Tangling in the wool.
	"Hand-woven," Elias said, his voice sounding further away, muffled by the cozy gloom. "Weight helps the spirit find the center of the bed."
	He stood and walked to the window, pulling the heavy indigo curtains shut. The sudden occlusion of the outside world made the cabin feel even smaller, a tiny, glowing ember in the heart of the wet forest.
	"I'll bank the fire," Elias said. "It'll hum all through the night."
	"Thank you, Elias," she sighed.
	"Don't thank the wood-keep," he replied softly. "Thank the rain. It’s the one singing the lullaby."
	The fire dimmed as he spread the ash, the bright orange fading to a deep, resonant red. The shadows in the corners stretched out, long and velvet-black, inviting the mind to rest. The rain continued its steady, ancient song, a liquid heartbeat that promised the world would be new and washed clean by morning. 
	Elara let her head fall back against the cushion. The scent of mint, the warmth of the oak, and the infinite rhythm of the water combined into a single, unbreakable cord of peace. She drifted, the cabin cradling her like a sturdy wooden nest, while the forest drank its fill in the dark.
