	The silver-birch antlers of Orion the Stag caught the first traces of starlight, casting long, swaying shadows across the mossy floor of the Whispering Woods. He stood ten feet tall at the shoulder, a living mountain of ancient cedar-scented fur and velvet moss. With every slow, deliberate exhale, a faint mist curled from his nostrils, dissipating into the cool evening air.
	Pip, a harvest mouse whose fur glowed with the soft luminescence of a firefly, adjusted his seat within the hollow of a silver-birch branch protruding from Orion’s head. He tucked a sprig of dried lavender under his chin and peered down at the forest floor.
	"The ground looks like a sea tonight, Orion," Pip whispered, his voice the sound of dry leaves skittering on stone.
	Orion tilted his massive head, the movement fluid and heavy. 
	"The shadows pool where the ferns are thickest," Orion replied. His voice rumbled deep in his chest, a low-frequency vibration that resonated through the trees. "Does the saddle of moss feel soft enough for the journey?"
	Pip patted the thick green cushion beneath him. 
	"It’s better than the finest silk in the kingdom," the mouse said. "It smells of rain and old secrets. Are we going to the Great Brook first?"
	Orion began to move. His hooves, wide and cushioned like the pads of a great cat, pressed into the earth without a sound. He didn't walk so much as flow through the undergrowth, his body weaving between the ancient oaks as if he were made of smoke.
	"The water calls for its nightly benediction," Orion said. "The stones need the silver light to wash away the heat of the day."
	They crested a small rise where the trees thinned, revealing a sky the color of crushed blueberries. A single owl glided overhead, its wings cutting the air with a faint *hush-hush* sound. 
	"Look at the Great Horned One," Pip said, pointing a tiny paw. "He’s late for the gathering."
	"He isn't late," Orion murmured. "The night moves at its own pace. Time here isn't a clock, Pip. It’s a tide."
	Pip leaned against the white bark of Orion’s antler. 
	"I sometimes wish I could see the tide," the mouse said. "I only see the grass swaying."
	"Close your eyes," Orion instructed. "Listen to the spruce needles rubbing against each other. That’s the tide. Listen to the sap slowing down in the maple trunks. That’s the clock stopping."
	Pip closed his eyes. The world transformed into a symphony of textures. He felt the rhythmic sway of Orion’s gait—a gentle, rocking motion that mimicked a boat on a glass-still lake. He heard the distant *plink-plop* of a frog slipping into a pond.
	"I hear it," Pip whispered. "It sounds like the forest is humming a song it forgot the words to."
	"It never forgets," Orion said. "It just prefers the melody to the lyrics."
	They descended into the valley where the Great Brook ran. The water didn't roar; it murmured, a continuous, liquid conversation between the current and the smooth, black stones. Orion stepped into the shallowest part, his legs stirring the reflection of the moon into a thousand shimmering shards.
	A large, flat-headed toad sat upon a submerged log, his skin mottled like old copper. He blinked slowly, his golden eyes reflecting the glowing mouse in Orion’s antlers.
	"Greetings, Weaver of the Path," the toad said, his voice a wet croak that felt like velvet.
	Orion bowed his head, the lanterns hanging from his birch-antlers swaying. 
	"Greetings, Barnaby," Orion said. "How does the water taste tonight?"
	Barnaby inflated his throat, the skin stretching thin and translucent. 
	"It tastes of mountain snow and the roots of the willow," the toad replied. "The current is slow. It’s a good night for drifting."
	Pip leaned over the edge of the antler-nest. 
	"Barnaby, did you see the dragonflies go to sleep?"
	The toad shifted his weight, the log bobbing gently. 
	"They’ve tucked themselves under the lily pads, little Pip. They’re dreaming of heat and gold. Why are you still awake? The moon is already high."
	"Orion is taking me to the Meadow of Moonlight," Pip explained. "I want to see the flowers open for the stars."
	"A fine destination," Barnaby said, his eyelids drooping. "But remember, the Meadow is a place for rest, not just for watching. Even the stars close their eyes when the dawn touches the rim."
	"We will be there soon," Orion said. "Sleep well, Keeper of the Stream."
	"Sleep... well..." Barnaby’s voice trailed off into a contented sigh as he settled deeper into his mossy perch.
	Orion waded out of the water, the moisture on his legs glistening like diamonds. He climbed the opposite bank, entering the Pine Cathedral. Here, the ground was a thick carpet of needles that muffled every sound. The air turned sharp and sweet with the scent of resin and cold earth. 
	"The air is heavier here," Pip noted, his small chest rising and falling in a slow, steady rhythm. "It feels like I'm breathing in a blanket."
	"The pines hold the breath of the day," Orion said. "They release it slowly to keep the night warm. Lean back, Pip. Let the needles sing to you."
	The wind moved through the high canopy of the pines, a sound like a long, drawn-out *shhhhh*. 
	"Why do the trees talk in whispers?" Pip asked, his voice growing smaller.
	"Because the secrets of the earth are too heavy for loud voices," Orion answered. "If they shouted, the mountains would crumble. So they whisper, and the mountains stay still."
	"Tell me a secret, Orion," Pip requested, stifling a yawn that stretched his tiny mouth wide.
	Orion’s hooves clicked softly against a stray stone. 
	"The secret is that the world never truly stops," the stag whispered. "Even when you sleep, the roots are reaching deeper. The stars are traveling millions of miles. The earth is turning in its sleep. You are never standing still, Pip. You are always being carried."
	"Carried by who?"
	"By the night. By the wind. By me."
	They passed through a grove of weeping willows whose branches trailed like long, green hair along the ground. The leaves brushed against Orion’s flanks with a sound like silk rubbing against silk.
	"Orion?" Pip asked after a long silence.
	"Yes, Little Light?"
	"Do the stars ever get tired of shining?"
	Orion paused beneath a gap in the canopy, looking up at the silver-white dusting above. 
	"They don't shine because they have to, Pip. They shine because they are full of light. When they get tired, they simply fall. They turn into streaks of dust and rest in the soil. That’s why the flowers grow so bright in the spring. They’re eating old starlight."
	Pip hummed a tiny, discordant note. 
	"I think I’m part star tonight. I feel very heavy. Like my paws are made of lead."
	"That is the weight of peace," Orion said. "It’s a good weight to carry."
	They reached the edge of the Meadow of Moonlight. It was a vast, rolling expanse of silver-tipped grass that moved in slow, rhythmic waves. In the center stood the Night-Blooming Jasmines, their petals unfurling like the fingers of a waking hand. A soft, pale mist clung to the ground, swirling around Orion’s knees.
	"We are here," Orion whispered.
	He knelt down, his movements slow and deliberate, lowering his body into the soft, tall grass. The Meadow smelled of honey and cold dew. 
	Pip crawled out of the birch-antler nest and scrambled down Orion’s velvet-covered neck. He landed in the thick moss near the stag’s ear.
	"It’s so bright," Pip said, staring at the jasmines. "They look like fallen moons."
	"They are the anchors of the night," Orion said, his head resting on a clump of soft clover. "They hold the darkness in place so the sun doesn't come too early."
	A group of fireflies began to dance around them, their light pulsing in time with Orion’s slow breathing. *Pulse. Fade. Pulse. Fade.*
	"Are you going to stay here?" Pip asked.
	"I will stay until the dew turns to frost," Orion said. "I will stay until the first lark sings. And then I will move into the deep shadows where the sun cannot find me."
	Pip curled into a ball, his tail wrapping around his nose. The heat from Orion’s body radiated outward like a warm hearth. 
	"Orion?"
	"Hush now, Little Light."
	"I just wanted to say... the tide is very quiet tonight."
	"It is," Orion agreed, his eyes half-closing. "The tide is exactly where it needs to be."
	The wind died down to a mere breath. The jasmines glowed with a soft, pulsing rhythm. The Whispering Woods fell into a deep, velvety silence, broken only by the rhythmic, steady heartbeat of the giant stag and the tiny, contented sighs of the mouse.
	"Sleep," Orion whispered, the word more a vibration than a sound. "The stars will watch the sky. The roots will watch the earth. I will watch the Meadow. There is nothing left to do but drift."
	Pip’s breathing synchronized with Orion’s. The world narrowed down to the scent of cedar, the softness of moss, and the slow, silver light of the moon.
	In the heart of the forest, the stag and the mouse became part of the landscape—a mound of moss and a spark of light, perfectly still, perfectly at rest, as the night turned slowly toward the dawn. The great birch-antlers stood like sentinels against the indigo sky, holding the lanterns of the woods until the last dream was finished.
	The silence grew deeper, a heavy, comfortable weight. The shadows lengthened, then merged. The Meadow of Moonlight exhaled a final, sweet perfume of jasmine. 
	All was still. All was quiet. All was well.
