	The scent of cured cedar and dried orange peel hung heavy in the air of the Great Divide Lodge. Outside, the sky had bruised into a deep, obsidian purple, swallowing the jagged peaks of the Tetons. Silas sat deep within the embrace of a wingback chair, his legs stretched toward the hearth where a massive river-stone fireplace dominated the room.
	The fire didn’t just burn; it breathed. A thick log of seasoned oak hissed as a bead of sap bubbled to the surface, popping with a sharp, rhythmic crack that echoed against the exposed rafters.
	Elias, the lodge keeper, moved through the shadows of the kitchenette with the practiced silence of a man who had spent forty years outrunning the wind. He carried a heavy ceramic teapot, steam curling from the spout in lazy ribbons.
	"The sleet is turning," Elias said, his voice a low vibration that seemed to come from the floorboards themselves. "Listen to the roof. That’s not the sharp peck of ice anymore."
	Silas tilted his head. The frantic drumming of the rain had softened. It was no longer a staccato beat against the shingles, but a muffled, rhythmic thrum—the sound of wet snow beginning to pile. 
	"It sounds heavy," Silas murmured.
	"The best kind," Elias replied. He set the teapot on a low, hand-hewn table between them. "It acts like a blanket. Give it an hour, and you won’t hear the wind at all. The mountain just goes quiet. Like it’s holding its breath."
	Elias poured the tea. The liquid was dark amber, smelling of valerian root and honey. He handed a mug to Silas, the warmth of the clay seeping into Silas’s palms instantly.
	"Drink that before the steam stops," Elias said. "It’ll settle the hum in your bones."
	Silas took a sip. The heat traveled down his throat, a slow-moving wave that unknotted the tension in his shoulders. 
	"You’ve stayed through a thousand of these, haven't you?" Silas asked.
	Elias sank into the opposite chair, his joints giving a faint, sympathetic creak to the settling wood of the lodge. 
	"More than I can count. Each one feels like the first time the world truly stops. You see how the firelight catches the frost on the windowpane?"
	Silas looked. At the edges of the glass, delicate crystalline ferns grew inward, illuminated by the orange pulse of the embers. Beyond the glass, the world had vanished into a swirling grey-white void. Huge, wet flakes drifted past the light, moving in slow, dizzying circles before vanishing into the dark.
	"It looks like ash," Silas said.
	"Cold ash," Elias agreed. "But look at the trees. The pines out there are sturdier than they look. They bend until their needles touch the drifts, then they just wait. They don't fight the weight. They let it happen."
	He took a slow draught of his tea, his eyes fixed on the dancing flames. 
	"People struggle against the quiet," Elias continued. "They think they have to fill it. But the mountain doesn’t care for noise. It’s got its own song."
	The wind groaned outside, a long, mournful sound that brushed against the heavy log walls. The lodge didn’t shudder; it felt solid, a fortress of ancient timber. Inside, the air remained still and thick with the warmth of the hearth.
	"What's the song tonight?" Silas asked.
	"Soft percussion," Elias said, gesturing toward the roof. "The snow is stacking. You can hear the weight of it if you listen close enough. It’s a rhythmic pressure. It’s the sound of the earth tucking itself in."
	Silas leaned back, the wool of his blanket scratching pleasantly against his neck. He watched a single spark rise from the grate, dancing upward into the black throat of the chimney. 
	"I feel like I'm drifting," Silas whispered.
	"That's the altitude," Elias said with a faint, knowing smile. "And the tea. And the fact that for the next twelve hours, there isn’t a single soul who can reach you. The road is a memory under three feet of powder now."
	"Does that ever frighten you?"
	Elias shook his head, the firelight reflecting in his silver hair. 
	"It’s the only time I feel entirely safe. When the world is locked away, you only have to be responsible for the space within reach of your fingertips. The fire. The tea. The breath in your lungs."
	Silas watched the shadows of the flames lick across the ceiling. The rough-hewn beams were stained dark, showing the marks of the adze that had shaped them a century ago. Every notch told a story of labor, of hands building a sanctuary against the very cold that now prowled outside.
	The sound on the roof changed again. It was a faint, continuous hiss now, like silk being dragged over velvet. 
	"The wind is dying down," Silas noted.
	"The storm is settling in for the night," Elias said. "It’s done its shouting. Now it’s just doing the work of covering the world. Look at the hearth, Silas. See the blue at the base of the flame?"
	Silas leaned forward slightly. At the core of the oak log, a brilliant, ghostly blue glow flickered, steady and intense.
	"That’s the heart of the wood," Elias murmured. "That’s the sun that grew the tree, coming back out after fifty years of being trapped in the grain. It’s a slow release. Everything in nature has its own clock."
	Silas felt his eyelids grow heavy. The rhythmic crackle of the fire and the muffled shush of the snow created a dual pulse, a slow metronome that seemed to sync with his own heartbeat.
	"I haven't felt this still in years," Silas admitted.
	"Most people spend their lives vibrating," Elias said softly. "Like a plucked string. But even a string needs to rest to stay in tune. You’re just finding your middle again."
	Elias stood up, his movement fluid and slow. He walked to the window and adjusted the heavy velvet curtains, leaving just a sliver of the silver-grey night visible. 
	"The mountain is going to sleep," Elias said, turning back. "You should do the same. The embers will hold until morning. I’ll put one more log of cherry on. It smells like a summer orchard when it burns."
	He picked up a gnarled piece of wood and placed it gently atop the glowing coals. Almost immediately, a sweet, fruity scent began to lace through the air, mixing with the musk of the oak and the sharp tang of the cedar.
	"Sleep is different up here," Elias said, his voice dropping to a near-whisper. "It’s deeper. You don't just close your eyes. You sink into the stone of the mountain itself."
	"Thank you, Elias," Silas said, his voice thick with the onset of rest.
	"Don't thank me. Thank the snow. It’s the one doing the heavy lifting."
	Elias moved toward the hallway, his footsteps fading into the soft rug. Silas was alone with the fire. He watched the new log catch, the tiny orange tongues licking around the bark, slowly claiming the wood. 
	The rhythm of the snow on the roof was a constant, gentle pressure, a lullaby of gravity and ice. Every few minutes, a soft *thump* would echo from outside—the sound of a tree shedding its white burden, the branch springing back up, relieved.
	Silas pulled the blanket tighter. The heat from the fireplace was a physical weight, a warm hand pressing against his chest. He watched the frost ferns on the window grow a fraction of an inch longer. 
	The world outside was a wilderness of white and shadow, but here, in the heart of the lodge, there was only the amber glow and the scent of cherry wood. The shadows on the walls performed a slow, graceful ballet, stretching and retreating in time with the flickering flames.
	He closed his eyes. Behind his lids, he could still see the orange pulse of the fire. The sound of the snow had become a part of him, a steady, hushing breath that told him there was nowhere else to go, nothing else to do, and no one else to be.
	The fire popped once more, a soft, final note. The cherry wood began to hiss its sweet song. Silas let out a long, slow breath, his mind drifting into the white silence of the mountain, wrapped in the safety of the wooden walls and the ancient, steady rhythm of the falling snow.
	Outside, the lodge sat like a small, glowing ember in a sea of ivory. The peaks watched over it, silent and eternal, as the snow continued its slow, graceful descent, draping the world in a thick, heavy peace that would last until the sun found its way back over the crest of the world. But for now, there was only the dark, the warmth, and the deep, uncomplicated quiet of the lodge.
